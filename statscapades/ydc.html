Whereas before there were no obvious trends, we can see now that the ratio slopes downward until day 40, when it seems to rise back up and gain some stability. We can test this hypothesis using a model with 2 distributions and finding the parameters using Bayesian estimation. To start, we convert the dates to days since the beginning of July (specifically, July 2nd). We then choose an exponential distribution as the prior, since ratios are continuous, and model the alpha parameter as another exponential variable, initialized to the mean ratio. The day of change should be modeled by a discrete uniform stochastic variable.

In [13]:
import pymc as pm 
lambda_1 = pm.Exponential("lambda_1", 1.0/i2q['ratio'].mean()) # priors
lambda_2 = pm.Exponential("lambda_2", 1.0/i2q['ratio'].mean())
tau = pm.DiscreteUniform("tau", lower=0, upper=61)

@pm.deterministic # lambda parameter is composed of lambda_1 and lambda_2
def lambda_(tau=tau, lambda_1=lambda_1, lambda_2=lambda_2):
    out = np.zeros(len(i2q['ratio']))
    out[:tau] = lambda_1
    out[tau:] = lambda_2
    return out
In [14]:
observation = pm.Exponential("obs", lambda_, value=i2q['ratio'], observed=True)
model = pm.Model([observation, lambda_1, lambda_2, tau])

mcmc = pm.MCMC(model)
mcmc.sample(40000, 10000, 1)

From the resulting posterior distributions, we can see that the estimates for lambda_1 and lambda_2 are both somewhere around 0.5. Also, the curves are wide, suggesting that the posterior estimate is uncertain. This indicates that there was no change in the invite-to-quote rate in the provided time period. The posterior distribution for tau supports this conclusion, since the distribution for most of the days is constant. The relatively higher probabilities on the first and last day again reinforces the conclusion that there was no change during this time period. The upward trend from around day 45 to the end of the time period could indicate a possible gradual increase in quote behavior, although it is hard to say definitely without more data.

Conclusion¶

Overall, there was not sufficient evidence to conclude that product changes had a change in site-wide quote behavior in the past two months. Additional tests are needed once more data from the next few months can be obtained. This could be because users and service providers are still adjusting to the new product changes, a process which may take several days or weeks, depending on the magnitude of the change.
